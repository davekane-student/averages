A Direct and Asymptotic Analysis of Coin Game Tie Probabilities

Author

Dave Kane (Student)

Published

July 1, 2026

1 Proposed Problem

Problem. Proposed by Dave Kane (Student).

Two players each flip their own coin \(F\) times. Player 1 got heads on each flip with success probability \(p_1\), and Player 2 with success probability \(p_2\). What is the probability \(P_{\text{tie}}(F)\) that both players end up with the exact same number of heads? Completely characterize both the exact closed-form representations and the asymptotic decay behavior of \(P_{\text{tie}}(F)\) as \(F \to \infty\) for all configurations of \(p_1\) and \(p_2\).

2 Solution

We present a comprehensive, self-contained solution. Since the coins are flipped independently, the probability of a tie is the sum of the joint probabilities of obtaining exactly \(k\) heads: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = \sum_{k=0}^{F} \binom{F}{k}^2 (p_1 p_2)^k \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{F-k}. \] We analyze this tie probability by partitioning the parameter space into three progressive regimes.

2.1 Case 1: The Fair Coin (\(p_1 = p_2 = 1/2\))

For fair coins, the success and failure probabilities are identical: \[ (p_1 p_2)^k \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{F-k} = \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^k \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^{F-k} = 4^{-F}. \] Since this term is independent of \(k\), we factor it out of the sum: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = 4^{-F} \sum_{k=0}^{F} \binom{F}{k}^2. \] To simplify the sum of squared binomial coefficients, we apply Vandermonde’s Identity: \[ \sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k} = \binom{m+n}{r}. \] Setting \(m = n = r = F\) and using the symmetry property \(\binom{F}{k} = \binom{F}{F-k}\): \[ \sum_{k=0}^{F} \binom{F}{k}^2 = \sum_{k=0}^{F} \binom{F}{k}\binom{F}{F-k} = \binom{2F}{F}. \] Substituting this back yields the exact, closed-form baseline: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = 4^{-F}\binom{2F}{F}. \]

2.1.1 Large-\(F\) Asymptotic Approximation

To find the asymptotic behavior, we apply Stirling’s approximation, \(n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n}\,(n/e)^n\), to both factorials in the binomial coefficient \(\binom{2F}{F} = \frac{(2F)!}{(F!)^2}\): \[ (2F)! \approx \sqrt{4\pi F}\,\frac{(2F)^{2F}}{e^{2F}}, \qquad (F!)^2 \approx 2\pi F\,\frac{F^{2F}}{e^{2F}}. \] Dividing these expressions: \[ \binom{2F}{F} \approx \frac{\sqrt{4\pi F} \cdot (2F)^{2F} / e^{2F}}{2\pi F \cdot F^{2F} / e^{2F}} = \frac{2\sqrt{\pi F} \cdot 4^F F^{2F}}{2\pi F \cdot F^{2F}} = \frac{4^F}{\sqrt{\pi F}}. \] Substituting this back cancels the \(4^{-F}\) term, revealing a polynomial decay of order \(O(F^{-1/2})\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi F}}. \]

2.1.2 Local Limit Theorem Justification of the \(\sqrt{\pi}\) Prefactor

To understand the origin of the \(\sqrt{\pi}\) term rigorously, we apply the de Moivre-Laplace Local Limit Theorem (LLT). Let \(S_F\) be the number of heads, which has mean \(\mu_F = F/2\) and variance \(F \sigma^2 = F/4\). The LLT states that: \[ \Pr(S_F = k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi F \sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{(k - F/2)^2}{2F\sigma^2}} + o\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{F}}\right). \] Since the support of \(S_F\) consists of integers with lattice spacing \(d = 1\), we can approximate the sum of squared probabilities as a Riemann sum. Let \(\Delta x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{F\sigma^2}}\) and \(x_k = \frac{k - F/2}{\sqrt{F\sigma^2}}\). Then: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = \sum_{k=0}^{F} \Pr(S_F = k)^2 \sim \sum_{k=0}^{F} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi F \sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{x_k^2}{2}} \right)^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi F \sigma^2} \sum_{k=0}^{F} e^{-x_k^2}. \] We factor out one copy of the lattice step size \(\Delta x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{F\sigma^2}}\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) \sim \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{F \sigma^2}} \sum_{k=0}^{F} e^{-x_k^2} \Delta x. \] In the limit \(F \to \infty\), the Riemann sum converges to the integral of a squared standard normal density: \[ \lim_{F \to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^{F} e^{-x_k^2} \Delta x = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}. \] Substituting \(\sigma = 1/2\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) \sim \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2\pi \sqrt{F \sigma^2}} = \frac{1}{2 \sigma \sqrt{\pi F}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi F}}. \] This formalizes the “collision probability” heuristic: the \(\sqrt{\pi}\) prefactor is the normalization constant of the Gaussian distribution, arising directly from integrating the squared density over a 1D lattice.

2.2 Case 2: Matched but Unfair Coins (\(p_1 = p_2 = p \neq 1/2\))

When the players use matching biased coins (\(p \neq 1/2\)), the term \(p^2 \neq (1-p)^2\), which prevents factoring out the probability term from the sum and breaks the simple Vandermonde collapse.

However, because the two probability distributions are centered at the identical mean \(F p\), their overlap remains maximized. By the Local Limit Theorem, the tie probability still decays polynomially as \(\Theta(F^{-1/2})\), with the variance determined by the biased coin’s parameter: \(\sigma^2 = p(1-p)\).

We can express the exact sum analytically in terms of classical Legendre Polynomials \(P_F(x)\) by mapping it to Gauss’s hypergeometric function \(_2F_1(-F, -F; 1; z)\) where \(z = \frac{p^2}{(1-p)^2}\), and applying a classical transformation identity:

Theorem 1. For two players flipping matching biased coins (\(p_1 = p_2 = p\)), the exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = (2p - 1)^F P_F\left( \frac{1 - 2p + 2p^2}{2p - 1} \right). \]

2.3 Case 3: General Asymmetric Coins (\(p_1 \neq p_2\))

When \(p_1 \neq p_2\), the two binomial distributions are centered at different expectations \(F p_1\) and \(F p_2\). This shifts the overlap of the distributions into their tail regions, driving the tie probability to decay exponentially.

2.3.1 Exact Analytical Asymptotic Formula via Laplace’s Method

We can derive the exact analytical asymptotic formula, including both the exponential rate and the polynomial prefactor, using Laplace’s method for sums.

Theorem 2. Let \(p_1 \neq p_2\). Define the positive constants: \[ A = \sqrt{p_1 p_2}, \qquad B = \sqrt{(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}. \] The asymptotic tie probability as \(F \to \infty\) is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) \sim \frac{A+B}{2\sqrt{\pi F AB}} (A+B)^{2F}. \]

Proof. We rewrite the exact sum by factoring: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = \sum_{k=0}^{F} \left[ \binom{F}{k} A^k B^{F-k} \right]^2. \] Let \(f(k) = \binom{F}{k} A^k B^{F-k}\). We wish to find the asymptotic sum \(\sum f(k)^2\). The peak of \(f(k)\) occurs at its expectation: \[ k^* = F \cdot p^*, \qquad \text{where } p^* = \frac{A}{A+B}, \ \ q^* = 1 - p^* = \frac{B}{A+B}. \] Applying Stirling’s approximation to the peak term \(f(k^*)\): \[ \binom{F}{F p^*} A^{F p^*} B^{F q^*} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi F p^* q^*}} (A+B)^F. \] Near the peak, we write \(k = F p^* + y \sqrt{F}\). The Gaussian approximation of the binomial term yields: \[ \binom{F}{k} A^k B^{F-k} \sim \frac{(A+B)^F}{\sqrt{2\pi F p^* q^*}} \exp\left( -\frac{(k - F p^*)^2}{2 F p^* q^*} \right). \] Squaring this term: \[ f(k)^2 \sim \frac{(A+B)^{2F}}{2\pi F p^* q^*} \exp\left( -\frac{(k - F p^*)^2}{F p^* q^*} \right). \] Summing \(f(k)^2\) over all \(k\) is asymptotically equivalent to integrating this Gaussian profile: \[ \sum_{k=0}^{F} f(k)^2 \sim \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{(A+B)^{2F}}{2\pi F p^* q^*} \exp\left( -\frac{y^2}{F p^* q^*} \right) dy. \] Evaluating this standard Gaussian integral: \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left( -\frac{y^2}{F p^* q^*} \right) dy = \sqrt{\pi F p^* q^*}. \] Multiplying the prefactors: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) \sim \frac{(A+B)^{2F}}{2\pi F p^* q^*} \cdot \sqrt{\pi F p^* q^*} = \frac{(A+B)^{2F}}{2\sqrt{\pi F p^* q^*}}. \] To simplify, we express \(p^* q^*\) in terms of \(A\) and \(B\): \[ p^* q^* = \left( \frac{A}{A+B} \right) \left( \frac{B}{A+B} \right) = \frac{AB}{(A+B)^2} \implies \sqrt{p^* q^*} = \frac{\sqrt{AB}}{A+B}. \] Substituting this into the denominator yields the stated formula: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) \sim \frac{(A+B)^{2F}}{2\sqrt{\pi F} \frac{\sqrt{AB}}{A+B}} = \frac{A+B}{2\sqrt{\pi F AB}} (A+B)^{2F}. \qquad \blacksquare \]

Since \(A+B = \sqrt{p_1 p_2} + \sqrt{(1-p_1)(1-p_2)} < 1\) for all \(p_1 \neq p_2\), this proves the exponential decay rate rigorously, while providing the exact polynomial prefactor \(\Theta(F^{-1/2})\).

2.3.2 Connection to Legendre Polynomials

Using the classical transformation identity between Gauss’s hypergeometric function \(_2F_1\) and Legendre Polynomials, we obtain the general exact closed form for any choice of \(p_1\) and \(p_2\):

Theorem 3. For any success probabilities \(p_1\) and \(p_2\), the exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = (p_1 + p_2 - 1)^F P_F\left( \frac{1 - p_1 - p_2 + 2 p_1 p_2}{p_1 + p_2 - 1} \right). \]

2.3.3 Proof of Removability at Complementarity (\(p_1 + p_2 = 1\))

Let \(u = p_1 + p_2 - 1\). The complementary condition is \(u = 0\). For \(u \neq 0\), the Legendre representation is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(F) = u^F P_F\left( \frac{1 - p_1 - p_2 + 2p_1p_2}{u} \right) = u^F P_F\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right). \] We can prove that the singularity at \(u = 0\) is removable and find its limit using two complementary methods: a direct algebraic expansion and an asymptotic limit analysis based on the leading-order behavior of Legendre Polynomials.

2.3.3.1 Method 1: Asymptotic Limit via Leading Legendre Terms

We recall that as the argument \(x \to \pm\infty\), the Legendre Polynomial \(P_F(x)\) of degree \(F\) is dominated by its leading-order term: \[ P_F(x) \sim \frac{1}{2^F} \binom{2F}{F} x^F. \] Under the complementarity condition, as the perturbation \(u = p_1+p_2-1 \to 0\), the argument \(x = \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u}\) grows without bound (\(x \to \pm\infty\)). Substituting this asymptotic behavior: \[ P_F\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right) \sim \frac{1}{2^F} \binom{2F}{F} \left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right)^F \sim \frac{1}{2^F} \binom{2F}{F} \frac{2^F (p_1 p_2)^F}{u^F} = \binom{2F}{F} \frac{(p_1 p_2)^F}{u^F}. \] Taking the limit as \(u \to 0\) of the full expression: \[ \lim_{u \to 0} u^F P_F\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right) = \lim_{u \to 0} u^F \left[ \binom{2F}{F} \frac{(p_1 p_2)^F}{u^F} \right] = \binom{2F}{F} (p_1 p_2)^F. \] Since \(p_1 + p_2 = 1 \implies p_2 = 1 - p_1\), this limits to \([p_1(1-p_1)]^F \binom{2F}{F}\), recovering the Vandermonde-collapsed baseline exactly.

2.3.3.2 Method 2: Exact Algebraic Cancellation

To verify that this holds exactly for all \(u \neq 0\) (and not just in the limit), we expand the Legendre Polynomial \(P_F(x)\) explicitly: \[ P_F(x) = \frac{1}{2^F} \sum_{j=0}^{F} \binom{F}{j}^2 (x-1)^{F-j} (x+1)^j. \] Substituting \(x = \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u}\) into the terms: \[ x - 1 = \frac{2p_1p_2 - 2u}{u} = \frac{2(p_1p_2 - (p_1+p_2-1))}{u} = \frac{2(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}{u}, \] \[ x + 1 = \frac{2p_1p_2}{u}. \] Substituting these back into the sum: \[ P_F\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right) = \frac{1}{2^F} \sum_{j=0}^{F} \binom{F}{j}^2 \left( \frac{2(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}{u} \right)^{F-j} \left( \frac{2p_1p_2}{u} \right)^j \] \[ = \frac{1}{2^F} \sum_{j=0}^{F} \binom{F}{j}^2 2^F \left( (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right)^{F-j} (p_1p_2)^j u^{-F} \] \[ = u^{-F} \sum_{j=0}^{F} \binom{F}{j}^2 (p_1p_2)^j \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{F-j}. \] Multiplying by \(u^F\), the \(u^{-F}\) cancels out completely, showing that for all \(u \neq 0\): \[ u^F P_F\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right) = \sum_{j=0}^{F} \binom{F}{j}^2 (p_1p_2)^j \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{F-j} = P_{\text{tie}}(F). \] This proves that the singularity at \(p_1 + p_2 = 1\) is indeed removable, and the limit as \(u \to 0\) converges exactly to the Vandermonde-collapsed baseline \([p_1(1-p_1)]^F \binom{2F}{F}\). This holds for all complementary probability pairs, including the fair-coin case (\(p_1=p_2=1/2\)). \(\blacksquare\)
