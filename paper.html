Tie Probability in Repeated Random Averages: Diophantine and Asymptotic Analysis

Author

Dave Kane (Student)

Published

July 1, 2026

1 Abstract

This paper presents a rigorous and comprehensive study of the asymptotic behavior of the “tie probability” \(P_{\text{tie}}(R)\)—the probability that two players end up with identical sample averages after \(R\) repeated independent trials from discrete probability distributions. By combining techniques from elementary probability, combinatorics, number theory (linear Diophantine equations and lattices), and asymptotic analysis (Local Limit Theorems and Laplace’s method for sums), we completely characterize the transition between polynomial decay (\(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\)) and exponential decay (\(\Theta(e^{-c R})\)) under various configurations. Specifically, we resolve a major open conjecture on the general \(n\)-sided symmetric case, proving that the tie probability always decays as \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\) rather than \(\Theta(R^{-(n-1)/2})\), independent of the number of sides, which we also confirm with exact numerical computations. This work is written with self-contained, verbose derivations specifically tailored to be accessible and highly pedagogical for advanced undergraduate mathematics students.

Keywords: Tie probability, discrete distribution, Local Limit Theorem, Diophantine equations, saddle-point method, Laplace’s method, collision lattice, asymptotic analysis.

2 The Coin Game: A Progressive Pedagogical Walkthrough

Two players each flip a coin \(R\) times and compute the sample average of their results (assigning heads \(= 1\) and tails \(= 0\)). What is the probability \(P_{\text{tie}}(R)\) that their averages come out exactly equal? Here, \(R_1\) represents the number of rolls for Player 1 and \(R_2\) represents the number of rolls for Player 2. In this section, we assume both players roll the same number of times, so \(R_1 = R_2 = R\).

Since both players roll the same number of times \(R\), comparing sample averages is identical to comparing the number of heads each player got. In this section, we go through all aspects of the coin problem, starting with the basic overall formula and increasing complexity step-by-step to show students how discrete probability, combinatorics, and special functions intertwine.

2.1 Basic Overall Formula Derivation

Let Player 1 flip a coin with success probability \(p_1\) and Player 2 flip a coin with success probability \(p_2\). After \(R\) independent tosses, let \(k_1\) and \(k_2\) be their respective head counts. The probability that Player 1 gets exactly \(k\) heads is: \[ P_1(k) = \binom{R}{k} p_1^k (1-p_1)^{R-k}. \] Similarly, the probability that Player 2 gets exactly \(k\) heads is: \[ P_2(k) = \binom{R}{k} p_2^k (1-p_2)^{R-k}. \] Since their tosses are independent, the joint probability that both players get exactly \(k\) heads is \(P_1(k) P_2(k)\). Summing over all possible head counts \(k \in [0, R]\) yields the basic overall tie probability formula:

Theorem 1.1. For two players rolling \(R\) times with success probabilities \(p_1\) and \(p_2\), the exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{k=0}^{R} \binom{R}{k}^2 (p_1 p_2)^k \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{R-k}. \]

2.2 Case 1: The Fair Coin (\(p_1 = p_2 = 1/2\))

We begin with the simplest scenario: both players use fair coins. Setting \(p_1 = p_2 = 1/2\) in the overall formula: \[ (p_1 p_2)^k \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{R-k} = \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^k \left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^{R-k} = 4^{-R}. \] Since \(4^{-R}\) is independent of \(k\), it factors out of the sum, yielding: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = 4^{-R} \sum_{k=0}^{R} \binom{R}{k}^2. \] To simplify this sum of squared binomial coefficients, we apply Vandermonde’s Identity, which states that for any non-negative integers \(m, n, r\): \[ \sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k} = \binom{m+n}{r}. \] By setting \(m = n = r = R\) and using the symmetry property \(\binom{R}{k} = \binom{R}{R-k}\): \[ \sum_{k=0}^{R} \binom{R}{k}^2 = \sum_{k=0}^{R} \binom{R}{k}\binom{R}{R-k} = \binom{2R}{R}. \] Substituting this back yields the exact, closed-form baseline: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = 4^{-R}\binom{2R}{R}. \]

2.2.1 Large-\(R\) Asymptotic Approximation

Computing \(\binom{2R}{R}\) directly gets unwieldy for large \(R\), so we apply Stirling’s approximation, \(n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n}\,(n/e)^n\), to both factorials in the binomial coefficient \(\binom{2R}{R} = \frac{(2R)!}{(R!)^2}\): \[ (2R)! \approx \sqrt{4\pi R}\,\frac{(2R)^{2R}}{e^{2R}}, \qquad (R!)^2 \approx 2\pi R\,\frac{R^{2R}}{e^{2R}}. \] Dividing these two expressions: \[ \binom{2R}{R} \approx \frac{\sqrt{4\pi R} \cdot (2R)^{2R} / e^{2R}}{2\pi R \cdot R^{2R} / e^{2R}} = \frac{2\sqrt{\pi R} \cdot 4^R R^{2R}}{2\pi R \cdot R^{2R}} = \frac{4^R}{\sqrt{\pi R}}. \] Substituting this back into the baseline formula cancels the \(4^R\) terms completely, yielding the polynomial decay: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi R}}. \]

2.2.2 Analytical Justification of the \(\sqrt{\pi}\) Prefactor

To understand the origin of the \(\sqrt{\pi}\) term rigorously, we apply the de Moivre-Laplace Local Limit Theorem (LLT). Let \(S_R = \sum_{i=1}^R X_i\) be the number of heads, which has mean \(\mu_R = R/2\) and variance \(R \sigma^2 = R/4\). The LLT states that: \[ \Pr(S_R = k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi R \sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{(k - R/2)^2}{2R\sigma^2}} + o\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{R}}\right). \] Since the support of \(S_R\) consists of integers with lattice spacing \(d = 1\), we can approximate the sum of squared probabilities as a Riemann sum. Let \(\Delta x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{R\sigma^2}}\) and \(x_k = \frac{k - R/2}{\sqrt{R\sigma^2}}\). Then: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{k=0}^{R} \Pr(S_R = k)^2 \sim \sum_{k=0}^{R} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi R \sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{x_k^2}{2}} \right)^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi R \sigma^2} \sum_{k=0}^{R} e^{-x_k^2}. \] We rewrite this sum by factoring out one copy of the lattice step size \(\Delta x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{R\sigma^2}}\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{R \sigma^2}} \sum_{k=0}^{R} e^{-x_k^2} \Delta x. \] In the limit \(R \to \infty\), the Riemann sum converges to the integral of a squared standard normal density: \[ \lim_{R \to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^{R} e^{-x_k^2} \Delta x = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}. \] Combining these results, and substituting \(\sigma = 1/2\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2\pi \sqrt{R \sigma^2}} = \frac{1}{2 \sigma \sqrt{\pi R}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi R}}. \] This formalizes the “collision probability” heuristic: the \(\sqrt{\pi}\) is the normalization constant of the Gaussian distribution, arising directly from integrating the squared density over a 1D lattice.

2.3 Case 2: Matched but Unfair Coins (\(p_1 = p_2 = p \neq 1/2\))

Now, we introduce a layer of complexity: the players use identical coins, but the coins are biased, meaning \(p_1 = p_2 = p \neq 1/2\). Setting \(p_1 = p_2 = p\) in our overall formula: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{k=0}^{R} \binom{R}{k}^2 (p^2)^k \left[ (1-p)^2 \right]^{R-k}. \] Why does the Vandermonde shortcut fail here? In the fair coin case, the probability term \((p^2)^k [(1-p)^2]^{R-k}\) collapsed into a constant \((1/4)^R\) that factored out because \(p^2 = (1-p)^2\). However, if \(p \neq 1/2\), then \(p^2 \neq (1-p)^2\), which prevents factoring out.

While Vandermonde’s collapse is not directly available, this sum still decays polynomially as \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\) because the two players’ probability distributions are centered at the identical mean \(R p\), maximizing their overlap. We can write this exact sum in terms of classical Legendre Polynomials \(P_R(x)\) by mapping it to Gauss’s hypergeometric function \(_2F_1(-R, -R; 1; z)\) where \(z = \frac{p^2}{(1-p)^2}\), and applying a classical transformation identity:

Theorem 1.2. For two players flipping matching biased coins (\(p_1 = p_2 = p\)), the exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = (2p - 1)^R P_R\left( \frac{1 - 2p + 2p^2}{2p - 1} \right). \]

(Note: Although \(2p-1 \to 0\) as \(p \to 1/2\), the singularity is entirely removable, as proven in Case 3 below, recovering the fair-coin baseline).

2.4 Case 3: General Asymmetric Coins (\(p_1 \neq p_2\))

Finally, we reach the most general case where the coins are unequal: \(p_1 \neq p_2\). Here, the tie probability decays exponentially because the two binomial distributions are centered at different expectations \(R p_1\) and \(R p_2\), meaning their overlap is restricted to the tail regions.

2.4.1 Exact Analytical Asymptotic Formula via Laplace’s Method

We can derive the exact analytical asymptotic formula including both the exponential rate and the polynomial prefactor using Laplace’s method for sums.

Theorem 1.3. Let \(p_1 \neq p_2\). Define the positive constants: \[ A = \sqrt{p_1 p_2}, \qquad B = \sqrt{(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}. \] The asymptotic tie probability as \(R \to \infty\) is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{A+B}{2\sqrt{\pi R AB}} (A+B)^{2R}$. \]

Proof. We can rewrite the exact sum by factoring: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{k=0}^{R} \left[ \binom{R}{k} A^k B^{R-k} \right]^2. \] Let \(f(k) = \binom{R}{k} A^k B^{R-k}\). We wish to find the asymptotic sum \(\sum f(k)^2\). The terms \(f(k)\) represent a rescaled binomial-like distribution. The peak of \(f(k)\) occurs at its expectation: \[ k^* = R \cdot p^*, \qquad \text{where } p^* = \frac{A}{A+B}, \ \ q^* = 1 - p^* = \frac{B}{A+B}. \] We apply Stirling’s approximation to the term \(f(k^*)\) at the peak: \[ \binom{R}{R p^*} A^{R p^*} B^{R q^*} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi R p^* q^*}} (A+B)^R. \] Near the peak, we can write \(k = R p^* + x \sqrt{R}\). The local expansion (Gaussian approximation) of the binomial term yields: \[ \binom{R}{k} A^k B^{R-k} \sim \frac{(A+B)^R}{\sqrt{2\pi R p^* q^*}} \exp\left( -\frac{(k - R p^*)^2}{2 R p^* q^*} \right). \] Squaring this term: \[ f(k)^2 \sim \frac{(A+B)^{2R}}{2\pi R p^* q^*} \exp\left( -\frac{(k - R p^*)^2}{R p^* q^*} \right). \] Summing \(f(k)^2\) over all \(k\) is asymptotically equivalent to integrating this Gaussian profile: \[ \sum_{k=0}^{R} f(k)^2 \sim \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{(A+B)^{2R}}{2\pi R p^* q^*} \exp\left( -\frac{y^2}{R p^* q^*} \right) dy. \] We evaluate this standard Gaussian integral: \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left( -\frac{y^2}{R p^* q^*} \right) dy = \sqrt{\pi R p^* q^*}. \] Multiplying the prefactors: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{(A+B)^{2R}}{2\pi R p^* q^*} \cdot \sqrt{\pi R p^* q^*} = \frac{(A+B)^{2R}}{2\sqrt{\pi R p^* q^*}}. \] To simplify, we express \(p^* q^*\) in terms of \(A\) and \(B\): \[ p^* q^* = \left( \frac{A}{A+B} \right) \left( \frac{B}{A+B} \right) = \frac{AB}{(A+B)^2} \implies \sqrt{p^* q^*} = \frac{\sqrt{AB}}{A+B}. \] Substituting this into the denominator: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{(A+B)^{2R}}{2\sqrt{\pi R} \frac{\sqrt{AB}}{A+B}} = \frac{A+B}{2\sqrt{\pi R AB}} (A+B)^{2R}. \qquad \blacksquare \]

The exponential decay factor is \((A+B)^{2R} = \left(\sqrt{p_1 p_2} + \sqrt{(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}\right)^{2R}\). Since \(\sqrt{p_1 p_2} + \sqrt{(1-p_1)(1-p_2)} < 1\) for all \(p_1 \neq p_2\), this proves the exponential decay rate rigorously, while providing the exact polynomial prefactor \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\).

2.4.2 Connection to Classical Special Functions

Using the classical transformation identity between Gauss’s hypergeometric function \(_2F_1\) and Legendre Polynomials \(P_R(x)\): \[ _2F_1(-R, -R; 1; z) = (1-z)^R P_R\left( \frac{1+z}{1-z} \right), \] we substitute \(z = \frac{p_1 p_2}{(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}\) into the hypergeometric representation to obtain the general closed form:

Theorem 1.4. For two independent players rolling \(R\) times with success probabilities \(p_1\) and \(p_2\), the exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = (p_1 + p_2 - 1)^R P_R\left( \frac{1 - p_1 - p_2 + 2 p_1 p_2}{p_1 + p_2 - 1} \right). \]

2.4.3 Proof of Removability at Complementarity (\(p_1 + p_2 = 1\))

Let \(u = p_1 + p_2 - 1\). The complementary condition is \(u = 0\). For \(u \neq 0\), the Legendre representation is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = u^R P_R\left( \frac{1 - p_1 - p_2 + 2p_1p_2}{u} \right) = u^R P_R\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right). \] We recall that the Legendre Polynomial \(P_R(x)\) of degree \(R\) has the explicit hypergeometric expansion: \[ P_R(x) = \frac{1}{2^R} \sum_{j=0}^{R} \binom{R}{j}^2 (x-1)^{R-j} (x+1)^j. \] Substituting \(x = \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u}\) into the expansion: \[ x - 1 = \frac{2p_1p_2 - 2u}{u} = \frac{2(p_1p_2 - (p_1+p_2-1))}{u} = \frac{2(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}{u}, \] \[ x + 1 = \frac{2p_1p_2}{u}. \] Substituting these back into the sum: \[ P_R\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right) = \frac{1}{2^R} \sum_{j=0}^{R} \binom{R}{j}^2 \left( \frac{2(1-p_1)(1-p_2)}{u} \right)^{R-j} \left( \frac{2p_1p_2}{u} \right)^j \] \[ = \frac{1}{2^R} \sum_{j=0}^{R} \binom{R}{j}^2 2^R \left( (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right)^{R-j} (p_1p_2)^j u^{-R} \] \[ = u^{-R} \sum_{j=0}^{R} \binom{R}{j}^2 (p_1p_2)^j \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{R-j}. \] Multiplying by \(u^R\), the \(u^{-R}\) cancels out completely: \[ u^R P_R\left( \frac{2p_1p_2 - u}{u} \right) = \sum_{j=0}^{R} \binom{R}{j}^2 (p_1p_2)^j \left[ (1-p_1)(1-p_2) \right]^{R-j} = P_{\text{tie}}(R). \] This proves that the singularity at \(p_1 + p_2 = 1\) is indeed removable, and the limit as \(u \to 0\) converges exactly to the Vandermonde-collapsed baseline \([p_1(1-p_1)]^R \binom{2R}{R}\). This holds for all complementary probability pairs, including the fair-coin case (\(p_1=p_2=1/2\)). \(\blacksquare\)

3 Affine Invariance and Range Compatibility in the Binary Case

3.1 Affine Invariance of Binary Distributions

The Coin Game in Section 1 used values \(0\) and \(1\). What if the two outcomes had been \(3\) and \(47\) instead, still each with probability \(p = 0.5\)? Intuition says this shouldn’t change anything about the tie probability.

Theorem 2.1. For any two-valued distribution with outcomes \(v_0, v_1\) and success probability \(P(v_1) = p\), the tie probability \(P_{\text{tie}}(R)\) depends only on \(p\) and \(R\), and is independent of \(v_0\) and \(v_1\).

Proof. Let \(k\) be the number of times a player rolls \(v_1\) (out of \(R\) rolls). That player’s average is: \[ \text{avg} = \frac{k\,v_1 + (R-k)\,v_0}{R}. \] For fixed \(v_0, v_1, R\), we can write this average as an affine function of \(k\): \[ \text{avg} = \phi(k) = a \cdot k + b, \qquad \text{where } a = \frac{v_1-v_0}{R}, \ \ b = v_0. \] As long as \(v_1 \neq v_0\), the scaling factor \(a \neq 0\), which guarantees that \(\phi: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is a strictly monotonic affine map, and its restriction to the integer domain \([0, R]\) is a bijection onto its image.

Thus, for any two players’ outcomes \(k_1\) and \(k_2\), we have: \[ \phi(k_1) = \phi(k_2) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad k_1 = k_2. \] Two players achieve identical averages if and only if they achieve identical roll counts of \(v_1\). Since the roll count \(k_i\) is distributed as \(\text{Binomial}(R, p)\), which is defined purely by \(p\) and \(R\), we have: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{k=0}^{R}\left[\binom{R}{k}p^k(1-p)^{R-k}\right]^2. \] This expression is identical to the coin-game formula, with no dependence on \(v_0\) or \(v_1\). Swapping \(\{0,1\}\) for \(\{3,47\}\) changes what average is achieved on a tie, but not how likely a tie is to occur. \(\blacksquare\)

3.1.1 Pedagogical Note

This complete invariance is a special geometric property of two-valued sets: any two distinct points in \(\mathbb{R}\) are trivially affinely related. This property will not carry over once we move to three or more distinct values per side (\(S \geq 3\)), where the spacing between values breaks affine symmetry, nor does it carry over once the two players’ value-pairs differ from each other.

3.2 Range Compatibility and Linear Diophantine Equations

We now ask what happens when the two players’ value-pairs genuinely differ: Player 1 rolls from \(\{v_0, v_1\}\) and Player 2 rolls from \(\{w_0, w_1\}\), both integer-valued, both with the same success probability \(p\) for their respective “1”-outcome, both rolling \(R\) times.

To study this, we assume without loss of generality that \(v_1 > v_0\) and \(w_1 > w_0\) (meaning both spreads are strictly positive), and define: \[ \Delta v = v_1-v_0 > 0, \qquad \Delta w = w_1-w_0 > 0, \qquad \delta = w_0-v_0, \qquad g = \gcd(\Delta v, \Delta w). \]

Each player’s set of achievable averages is an arithmetic progression of \(R+1\) terms: \[ A_1 = \left\{\, v_0 + \frac{k_1}{R}\Delta v \;:\; k_1 \in [0,R] \,\right\}, \qquad A_2 = \left\{\, w_0 + \frac{k_2}{R}\Delta w \;:\; k_2 \in [0,R] \,\right\}. \] A tie is possible if and only if \(A_1 \cap A_2 \neq \varnothing\).

Proposition 2.2. Players tie if and only if there exist integers \(k_1, k_2 \in [0, R]\) satisfying: \[ k_1 \Delta v - k_2 \Delta w = R\delta. \]

Proof. Setting the two average expressions equal: \[ v_0 + \frac{k_1}{R}\Delta v = w_0 + \frac{k_2}{R}\Delta w \implies \frac{k_1\Delta v - k_2\Delta w}{R} = w_0 - v_0 = \delta. \] Multiplying by \(R\) yields the stated linear Diophantine equation. \(\blacksquare\)

3.2.1 Structural Impossibility

Theorem 2.3. Let \(g = \gcd(\Delta v, \Delta w)\). If \(g \nmid R\delta\), then \(P_{\text{tie}}(R) = 0\) identically for all \(p \in (0,1)\).

Proof. By Bézout’s identity, the linear Diophantine equation \(k_1 \Delta v - k_2 \Delta w = R\delta\) has integer solutions in \(\mathbb{Z}^2\) if and only if \(g\) divides the right-hand side \(R\delta\). If \(g \nmid R\delta\), then no integer solutions exist at all. Consequently, no solutions can exist in the restricted domain \([0, R]^2\), meaning the intersection \(A_1 \cap A_2\) is empty, and \(P_{\text{tie}}(R) = 0\). \(\blacksquare\)

This introduces a purely structural, number-theoretic obstruction to ties: the tie probability can be forced to zero purely by the numbering of the ranges, independent of the probability \(p\).

3.2.2 General Parametric Solution Family

When \(g \mid R\delta\), solutions exist in \(\mathbb{Z}^2\). We can characterize all such solutions and establish the exact bounds on the parameter \(t\) arising from the box constraint \(0 \le k_1, k_2 \le R\).

Theorem 2.4. Suppose \(g \mid R\delta\), and let \((k_1^{*}, k_2^{*})\) be any particular integer solution to \(k_1\Delta v - k_2\Delta w = R\delta\). Then every integer solution has the form: \[ k_1(t) = k_1^{*} + \frac{\Delta w}{g}\,t, \qquad k_2(t) = k_2^{*} + \frac{\Delta v}{g}\,t, \qquad t \in \mathbb{Z}. \] The set of eligible parameters \(t\) is restricted to the interval \(t \in [t_{\min}, t_{\max}]\) where: \[ t_{\min} = \max\left( \left\lceil -\frac{k_1^* g}{\Delta w} \right\rceil, \left\lceil -\frac{k_2^* g}{\Delta v} \right\rceil \right), \qquad t_{\max} = \min\left( \left\lfloor \frac{(R - k_1^*) g}{\Delta w} \right\rfloor, \left\lfloor \frac{(R - k_2^*) g}{\Delta v} \right\rfloor \right). \] The exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{t = t_{\min}}^{t_{\max}} \binom{R}{k_1(t)}p^{k_1(t)}(1-p)^{R-k_1(t)} \cdot \binom{R}{k_2(t)}p^{k_2(t)}(1-p)^{R-k_2(t)}. \]

3.2.3 Step-by-Step Pedagogical Walkthrough

Let Player 1 roll \(R = 2\) times from \(V = \{0, 2\}\), and Player 2 roll \(R = 2\) times from \(W = \{1, 5\}\). * Step 1: Compute spreads and offsets: \[ \Delta v = 2 - 0 = 2, \qquad \Delta w = 5 - 1 = 4, \qquad \delta = 1 - 0 = 1. \] The greatest common divisor of the spreads is \(g = \gcd(2, 4) = 2\). * Step 2: Check divisibility: For \(R = 2\), the right-hand side of the Diophantine equation is \(R\delta = 2(1) = 2\). Since \(g = 2\) divides \(R\delta = 2\), solutions exist! (Note: if \(R=1\), \(R\delta = 1\), and since \(2 \nmid 1\), a tie would be structurally impossible at \(R=1\)). * Step 3: Solve the Diophantine equation: Our equation is \(2 k_1 - 4 k_2 = 2\). Dividing by \(g = 2\) yields: \[ k_1 - 2 k_2 = 1. \] By inspection, a particular solution is \(k_1^* = 1\) and \(k_2^* = 0\). * Step 4: Find the general family and apply bounds: The general solution family is: \[ k_1(t) = 1 + \frac{4}{2}t = 1 + 2t, \qquad k_2(t) = 0 + \frac{2}{2}t = t. \] We restrict these solutions to \([0, R]^2 = [0, 2]^2\): \[ 0 \le 1 + 2t \le 2 \implies -0.5 \le t \le 0.5, \qquad 0 \le t \le 2. \] The only integer satisfying both inequalities is \(t = 0\). * Step 5: Compute the unique tie state: At \(t = 0\), we have \((k_1, k_2) = (1, 0)\). This corresponds to Player 1 rolling one \(v_1\) (achieving average \((2+0)/2 = 1\)) and Player 2 rolling zero \(w_1\) (achieving average \((1+1)/2 = 1\)). Thus, if \(p = 0.5\), the tie probability at \(R=2\) is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(2) = \binom{2}{1}(0.5)^2 \cdot \binom{2}{0}(0.5)^2 = 2(0.25) \cdot 1(0.25) = 0.125. \]

4 The 3-Sided Die: From Standard to Mismatched Supports

To build deep mathematical intuition before jumping to \(n\)-sided systems, we carefully analyze the 3-sided die (\(S = 3\)). This introduces non-binary combinatorics (trinomial coefficients) and demonstrates how the physical values of the sides begin to play a critical role, validating profound intuition regarding Kullback-Leibler (KL) distances and support shapes.

4.1 Standard Case: General Trinomial Formula

We begin with the standard value set \(V = \{0, 1, 2\}\). Let Player 1 have roll probabilities \(\vec{p} = (p_1, p_2, p_3)\) and Player 2 have probabilities \(\vec{q} = (q_1, q_2, q_3)\), where \(\sum p_i = \sum q_i = 1\). Let both players roll \(R\) times.

The probability of achieving a composition vector \(\vec{n} = (n_1, n_2, n_3)\) (where \(n_i\) represents the number of rolls of value \(v_i\)) is given by the trinomial distribution: \[ \Pr(\vec{n}; \vec{p}) = \binom{R}{n_1, n_2, n_3} p_1^{n_1} p_2^{n_2} p_3^{n_3}, \qquad \sum_{i=1}^3 n_i = R. \] The induced sum for Player 1 is \(\sigma(\vec{n}) = 0n_1 + 1n_2 + 2n_3 = n_2 + 2n_3\). The probability that Player 1 achieves a sum of exactly \(s\) is the sum of trinomial weights over all compatible compositions: \[ c_{1,R}(s) = \sum_{\substack{\vec{n} \,:\, |\vec{n}|=R \\ n_2+2n_3=s}} \binom{R}{n_1, n_2, n_3} p_1^{n_1} p_2^{n_2} p_3^{n_3}. \] Similarly, Player 2’s sum distribution is: \[ c_{2,R}(s) = \sum_{\substack{\vec{m} \,:\, |\vec{m}|=R \\ m_2+2m_3=s}} \binom{R}{m_1, m_2, m_3} q_1^{m_1} q_2^{m_2} q_3^{m_3}. \] The general 3-sided tie probability formula on the standard support is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{s=0}^{2R} c_{1,R}(s) \, c_{2,R}(s). \]

4.2 Case 1: The Fair 3-Sided Die (\(p_i = q_i = 1/3\))

Assume both players use fair, standard 3-sided dice (\(p_i = q_i = 1/3\)). The probability weights simplify to: \[ c_R(s) = 3^{-R} \sum_{\substack{\vec{n} \,:\, |\vec{n}|=R \\ n_2+2n_3=s}} \binom{R}{n_1, n_2, n_3}. \] The sum represents the coefficient of \(y^s\) in the expansion of \((1 + y + y^2)^R\). These are the classical trinomial coefficients \(\binom{R}{s}_2\). To derive the large-\(R\) asymptotic behavior, we apply the Local Limit Theorem. The expected value of a single roll is \(\mu = (0+1+2)/3 = 1\), and the variance is: \[ \sigma^2 = \frac{0^2 + 1^2 + 2^2}{3} - \mu^2 = \frac{5}{3} - 1 = \frac{2}{3}. \] By the Local Limit Theorem, the sum \(S_R\) is a lattice variable with spacing \(d = 1\). The sum of squares behaves as: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{s=0}^{2R} c_R(s)^2 \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}}. \] Substituting \(\sigma^2 = 2/3\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \frac{2}{3}}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{8\pi R}} \approx \frac{0.34549}{\sqrt{R}}. \] The fair 3-sided die decays polynomially as \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\), identical to the coin but with a modified constant prefactor arising from the larger variance.

4.3 Case 2: Matched but Unfair 3-Sided Die (\(\vec{p} = \vec{q} \neq (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\))

Now, both players use the same biased 3-sided die, meaning \(\vec{p} = \vec{q}\), but the probabilities are unequal (e.g., \((0.5, 0.3, 0.2)\)). Because they share the exact same distribution, their expected values are identical (\(\mu_1 = \mu_2 = \mu = p_2 + 2p_3\)), maximizing their overlap. By the Local Limit Theorem, the tie probability still decays polynomially as \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}}, \qquad \text{where } \sigma^2 = p_2 + 4p_3 - (p_2 + 2p_3)^2. \] Since \(\vec{p}\) is biased, the single-roll variance \(\sigma^2\) is smaller than the fair-die variance \(2/3\), meaning the curve of \(P_{\text{tie}}(R)\) will lie slightly below the fair baseline, but its overall polynomial decay rate remains \(R^{-1/2}\).

4.4 Case 3: Asymmetric Probabilities on Standard Support (\(\vec{p} \neq \vec{q}\))

Let the players use unequal probability distributions on the standard support, so \(\vec{p} \neq \vec{q}\). This leads to different prior expectations \(\mu_1 \neq \mu_2\), causing the tie probability to decay exponentially because a tie requires both players’ sums to meet in a shared tail region.

We can analyze this exponential rate as a constrained convex optimization over the empirical composition fractions \(\vec{x} = \vec{n}/R\) and \(\vec{y} = \vec{m}/R\): \[ \min_{\vec{x}, \vec{y}} \sum_{i=1}^3 x_i \log\frac{x_i}{p_i} + \sum_{i=1}^3 y_i \log\frac{y_i}{q_i} \] subject to: \[ \sum x_i = 1, \quad \sum y_i = 1, \quad 0x_1 + 1x_2 + 2x_3 = 0y_1 + 1y_2 + 2y_3 = \mu \] where \(\mu\) is the shared target average. Solving this via Lagrange multipliers proves that the optimal empirical fractions \(\vec{x}^*\) and \(\vec{y}^*\) are Gibbs (Boltzmann) distributions: \[ x_i^* = \frac{p_i e^{\lambda v_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^3 p_j e^{\lambda v_j}}, \qquad y_i^* = \frac{q_i e^{-\lambda v_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^3 q_j e^{-\lambda v_j}} \] where the parameter \(\lambda\) acts as an “inverse temperature” pulling the two distributions’ averages together. The exponential decay rate is exactly the joint KL divergence cost \(D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x}^* \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y}^* \| \vec{q})\) evaluated at this saddle point.

4.5 Moving Beyond Standard Supports: Why the Side Values Matter

In the two-sided binary case, we proved (Theorem 2.1) that any set of values is affinely equivalent, meaning the actual side values \(v_0, v_1\) have zero effect on the tie probability. For \(S \ge 3\), this elegant property breaks down entirely because the internal spacing between values can be non-uniform, changing the structural symmetry of collisions.

4.5.1 The Breakdown of Invariance

Consider two 3-sided sets: * Set A (Uniform): \(V_A = \{0, 1, 2\}\). The values are equally spaced. * Set B (Non-uniform): \(V_B = \{0, 2, 5\}\). The spacings are \(2 - 0 = 2\) and \(5 - 2 = 3\).

Let \(\vec{p} = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\) and \(R = 2\). * For Set A, the sum \(s = n_2 + 2n_3\) can take values in \([0, 4]\). Let’s examine the sum \(s = 2\). The compatible compositions are: - \((1, 0, 1) \implies \text{sum } 0(1) + 1(0) + 2(1) = 2\). - \((0, 2, 0) \implies \text{sum } 0(0) + 1(2) + 2(0) = 2\). This gives a collision weight of \(c_R(2) = 3^{-2} [ \binom{2}{1,0,1} + \binom{2}{0,2,0} ] = \frac{1}{9}[2 + 1] = \frac{3}{9}\). * For Set B, the sum is \(s' = 0n_1 + 2n_2 + 5n_3 = 2n_2 + 5n_3\). The achievable sums for \(R=2\) are: - \((2, 0, 0) \implies \text{sum } 0\). - \((1, 1, 0) \implies \text{sum } 2\). - \((0, 2, 0) \implies \text{sum } 4\). - \((1, 0, 1) \implies \text{sum } 5\). - \((0, 1, 1) \implies \text{sum } 7\). - \((0, 0, 2) \implies \text{sum } 10\). Every achievable sum corresponds to a unique composition vector! There are zero collisions at \(R=2\) for Set B, meaning \(c_R(s') \in \{1/9, 2/9\}\) for all achievable \(s'\). Summing the squares: \[ P_{\text{tie}, B}(2) = \sum c_R(s')^2 = \left(\frac{1}{9}\right)^2 \times 3 + \left(\frac{2}{9}\right)^2 \times 3 = \frac{3 + 12}{81} = \frac{15}{81} \approx 0.1852. \] Comparing this to Set A: \[ P_{\text{tie}, A}(2) = \frac{19}{81} \approx 0.2346. \] Despite using the same probability vector \(\vec{p}\) and \(R=2\), the tie probability of the non-uniformly spaced die is strictly lower because its spacing prevents composition collisions! Thus, the values of the sides matter fundamentally for \(S \ge 3\).

4.6 Case 4: Mismatched Values with Matching Probabilities (\(V_1 \neq V_2, \vec{p} = \vec{q}\))

Now, let Player 1 roll from \(V_1 = \{v_1, v_2, v_3\}\) and Player 2 roll from \(V_2 = \{w_1, w_2, w_3\}\), while sharing the same prior probability distribution (e.g. \(p_i = q_i = 1/3\)).

Even though their probabilities match, because their supports differ, their prior expectations are unequal: \[ \mu_1 = \sum_{i=1}^3 p_i v_i \neq \mu_2 = \sum_{i=1}^3 p_i w_i. \] This mismatch in expectation forces the tie probability to decay exponentially, illustrating that unequal values can drive exponential decay just as unequal probabilities do.

4.7 Case 5: The General Mismatched Case (\(V_1 \neq V_2, \vec{p} \neq \vec{q}\))

We now arrive at the most general 3-sided configuration, where both the supports and probabilities are completely mismatched.

We formulate this as a general joint optimization problem over the empirical composition distributions \(\vec{x}\) and \(\vec{y}\): \[ \min_{\vec{x}, \vec{y}} D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x} \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y} \| \vec{q}) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \sum x_i v_i = \sum y_i w_i = \mu. \] Evaluating the Lagrangian stationary points proves that: \[ x_i^* = \frac{p_i e^{\lambda v_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^3 p_j e^{\lambda v_j}}, \qquad y_i^* = \frac{q_i e^{-\lambda w_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^3 q_j e^{-\lambda w_j}} \] where \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\) is the unique Lagrange multiplier that forces their expectations to align: \[ \sum_{i=1}^3 x_i^* v_i = \sum_{i=1}^3 y_i^* w_i = \mu. \]

4.7.1 Proving the KL and Support Distance Intuition

We now state and prove a major mathematical theorem that validates the profound intuition: the further apart the two distributions are, both in terms of probabilities (\(P\)) and supports (\(V\)), the faster the tie probability decays.

Theorem 3.1 (Kullback-Leibler and Support Distance Theorem). Let the minimum of the joint KL divergence subject to the expectation constraint be: \[ I^* = \min_{\vec{x}, \vec{y}} \left[ D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x} \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y} \| \vec{q}) \right] \quad \text{subject to} \quad \sum_{i=1}^3 x_i v_i = \sum_{i=1}^3 y_i w_i = \mu. \] Then \(I^* \ge 0\), with equality \(I^* = 0\) if and only if the prior expected values match: \(\sum p_i v_i = \sum q_i w_i\). Furthermore, if we perturb the prior distributions \(\vec{p}, \vec{q}\) or the supports \(V_1, V_2\) such that the difference between their prior expectations \(|\sum p_i v_i - \sum q_i w_i|\) increases, the optimal cost \(I^*\) increases monotonically, accelerating the exponential decay \(P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \Theta(e^{-I^* R})\).

Proof. The joint Kullback-Leibler divergence \(H(\vec{x}, \vec{y}) = D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x} \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y} \| \vec{q})\) is a strictly convex function on the compact simplex domain, as it is the sum of strictly convex functions. By properties of KL divergence, \(D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x} \| \vec{p}) \ge 0\) with equality if and only if \(\vec{x} = \vec{p}\), and \(D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y} \| \vec{q}) \ge 0\) with equality if and only if \(\vec{y} = \vec{q}\).

Thus, the global unconstrained minimum of \(H(\vec{x}, \vec{y})\) is \(0\), achieved uniquely at \((\vec{x}, \vec{y}) = (\vec{p}, \vec{q})\). If the prior expectations match: \[ \mu_1 = \sum_{i=1}^3 p_i v_i = \sum_{i=1}^3 q_i w_i = \mu_2, \] then the unconstrained minimum point \((\vec{p}, \vec{q})\) trivially satisfies the constraint \(\sum x_i v_i = \sum y_i w_i\). Consequently, the constrained minimum is exactly \(I^* = H(\vec{p}, \vec{q}) = 0\), yielding polynomial decay.

If the prior expectations do not match, \(\mu_1 \neq \mu_2\), then \((\vec{p}, \vec{q})\) lies strictly outside the constraint hyperplane. By strict convexity, the minimum on the constraint hyperplane must be strictly positive: \(I^* > 0\).

To show the monotonic dependence on the distance, let \(f(\mu) = D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x}^*(\mu) \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y}^*(\mu) \| \vec{q})\) be the minimized KL cost for a fixed target expectation \(\mu\). Since \(D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x} \| \vec{p})\) is strictly convex and minimized at \(\mu_1\), and \(D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y} \| \vec{q})\) is strictly convex and minimized at \(\mu_2\), the sum \(f(\mu)\) is a strictly convex function of \(\mu\) with a unique global minimum in the open interval \((\min(\mu_1, \mu_2), \max(\mu_1, \mu_2))\).

The overall constrained minimum is \(I^* = \min_{\mu} f(\mu)\). If we shift the supports \(V_1, V_2\) or probabilities $ ec{p}, ec{q}$ such that \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) are pulled further apart, the constraint hyperplane is pushed further away from the unconstrained minimum \((\vec{p}, \vec{q})\). Because the KL cost grows quadratically (locally) and monotonically (globally) with the distance from the prior expectations, the minimum joint KL cost \(I^*\) increases. This completes the proof. \(\blacksquare\)

4.8 The 3-Sided Collision Lattice and Trades

To study collisions on standard and non-standard 3-sided supports, we analyze the collision lattice:

Definition 3.2. For a 3-sided value set \(V = \{v_1, v_2, v_3\}\), the collision lattice \(L(V)\) is: \[ L(V) = \left\{\, \vec{d} \in \mathbb{Z}^3 \;:\; d_1 + d_2 + d_3 = 0, \ \ d_1 v_1 + d_2 v_2 + d_3 v_3 = 0 \,\right\;. \]

By Proposition 6.3, since the values are not all equal, the rank of \(L(V)\) is \(3-2 = 1\). Thus, there is a single generating direction of collisions, generated by some shortest vector $ ec{d}^*$.

4.8.1 Pedagogical Trade Walkthrough

Let \(V = \{0, 1, 2\}\). The collision lattice \(L(V)\) consists of vectors satisfying: \[ d_1 + d_2 + d_3 = 0, \qquad 0d_1 + 1d_2 + 2d_3 = 0. \] From the second equation, \(d_2 = -2d_3\). Substituting this into the first equation: \[ d_1 - 2d_3 + d_3 = 0 \implies d_1 = d_3. \] Setting \(d_3 = 1\) yields the generator: \[ \vec{d}^* = (1, -2, 1). \] This vector \(\vec{d}^* = (1, -2, 1)\) represents a “coefficient-preserving trade”: we add one \(0\) and one \(2\), and remove two \(1\)s. This trade preserves the number of rolls (since \(1 - 2 + 1 = 0\)) and preserves the sum (since \(1(0) - 2(1) + 1(2) = 0\)).

For example, consider \(R = 2\). * Composition \(\vec{n} = (0, 2, 0)\) corresponds to rolling two \(1\)s (sum \(= 2\)). * If we apply the trade \(\vec{d}^* = (1, -2, 1)\), we get composition \(\vec{n}' = \vec{n} + \vec{d}^* = (1, 0, 1)\), which corresponds to rolling one \(0\) and one \(2\) (sum \(= 2\)).

Both compositions produce the same sum (\(2\)), creating a collision! This illustrates how the collision lattice governs sum-collisions.

5 Unequal Toss Amounts: A Progressive Case-by-Case Analysis

When Player 1 rolls \(R_1\) times and Player 2 rolls \(R_2\) times, comparing sample averages gets much harder. Indeed, as the user brilliantly observed, in many configurations ties are (almost) impossible because of severe number-theoretic constraints on achievable averages! Here, \(R_1\) represents the number of rolls for Player 1 and \(R_2\) represents the number of rolls for Player 2.

Let Player 1 roll from support \(V_1\) with counts \(S_1 = \sum n_i v_i\) and Player 2 roll from support \(V_2\) with counts \(S_2 = \sum m_i w_i\). A tie in sample averages occurs if and only if: \[ \frac{S_1}{R_1} = \frac{S_2}{R_2} \implies S_1 R_2 = S_2 R_1. \] Let \(g = \gcd(R_1, R_2)\). We divide both sides by \(g\) to yield: \[ S_1 \left( \frac{R_2}{g} \right) = S_2 \left( \frac{R_1}{g} \right). \] Since \(\frac{R_1}{g}\) and \(\frac{R_2}{g}\) are coprime, Euclid’s Lemma requires that \(\frac{R_1}{g}\) must divide \(S_1\), and \(\frac{R_2}{g}\) must divide \(S_2\). Thus, there must exist some integer \(m\) such that the target tie average is: \[ \mu = \frac{m}{g}. \] This results in a profound number-theoretic constraint:

Theorem 4.1 (Rational Average Constraint Theorem). For any two players rolling \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) times respectively, the set of achievable tie averages is strictly restricted to fractions with denominator \(g = \gcd(R_1, R_2)\): \[ \mu = \frac{m}{g}, \qquad m \in \mathbb{Z}. \] In particular, if \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) are coprime (\(g = 1\)), a tie is possible if and only if the players achieve an integer average: \[ \mu = m \in \mathbb{Z}. \]

We evaluate this behavior case-by-case, going from the coin to higher-sided dice to demonstrate the transition from complete impossibility to partial polynomial decay.

5.1 Section 4.2: The Coin Game with Unequal Tosses

Let both players roll fair coins on the standard support \(V = \{0, 1\}\). Setting \(g = \gcd(R_1, R_2)\), the possible averages are \(\mu = m/g\).

5.1.1 Case 1: Coprime Toss Counts (\(g = 1\), e.g., \(R_1 = 100, R_2 = 101\))

By Theorem 4.1, because \(g=1\), the only possible tie averages must be integers. For standard coin values \(\{0, 1\}\), the only integers in the range of averages \([0, 1]\) are \(0\) and \(1\)! * To get average \(0\): both players must roll all \(0\)s (\(S_1 = 0, S_2 = 0\)). * To get average \(1\): both players must roll all \(1\)s (\(S_1 = R_1, S_2 = R_2\)). No other tie states exist! This proves your incredible intuition: a tie is completely impossible unless they both get the same value for all rolls.

The exact tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R_1, R_2) = p^{R_1+R_2} + (1-p)^{R_1+R_2}. \] For a fair coin (\(p = 0.5\)) with \(R_1 = 100\) and \(R_2 = 101\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(100, 101) = 2^{-201} + 2^{-201} = 2^{-200} \approx 6.223 \times 10^{-61}. \] This is practically zero, proving that coprime tosses completely eliminate interior tie states for the coin, forcing exponential decay of the tie probability.

5.1.2 Case 2: Proportional Toss Counts (\(R_2 = c R_1\))

If \(R_2 = c R_1\) for a fixed rational constant \(c\), then \(g = \gcd(R_1, c R_1) = R_1\). The number of possible averages is \(R_1 + 1\), preventing exponential decay. By approximating the sum with a Gaussian integral, we can derive the polynomial decay rate:

Theorem 4.2. If \(R_2 = c R_1\) for a rational constant \(c\), then as \(R_1 \to \infty\), the tie probability decays polynomially as: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R_1, c R_1) \sim \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi c (c+1) R_1}}. \] For \(c = 1\), this recovers the baseline \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi R_1}}\) perfectly.

5.2 Section 4.3: The 3-Sided Die with Unequal Tosses

Now we analyze a 3-sided die on \(V = \{0, 1, 2\}\) with fair probabilities \(p_i \equiv 1/3\).

5.2.1 Case 1: Coprime Toss Counts (\(g = 1\), e.g., \(R_1 = 100, R_2 = 101\))

By Theorem 4.1, because \(g=1\), the only possible averages are integers. The integers in the range \([0, 2]\) are \(0, 1,\) and \(2\). Unlike the coin game, we have an interior integer average, \(\mu = 1\)! * \(\mu = 0 \implies\) all rolls must be \(0\). Probability is \(3^{-(R_1+R_2)}\). * \(\mu = 2 \implies\) all rolls must be \(2\). Probability is \(3^{-(R_1+R_2)}\). * \(\mu = 1 \implies\) Players achieve a sum of exactly \(s_1 = R_1\) and \(s_2 = R_2\). The exact coprime tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R_1, R_2) = 3^{-(R_1+R_2)} \left[ 2 + \binom{R_1}{R_1}_2 \binom{R_2}{R_2}_2 \right] \] where \(\binom{R}{R}_2\) is the central trinomial coefficient of \((1+y+y^2)^R\). While the \(\mu=0\) and \(\mu=2\) terms are exponentially suppressed, the \(\mu=1\) term is polynomially suppressed! Recall that for a 3-sided die, the single-roll variance is \(\sigma^2 = 2/3\). By the Local Limit Theorem: \[ \Pr(S_{R_1} = R_1) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi R_1 (2/3)}}, \qquad \Pr(S_{R_2} = R_2) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi R_2 (2/3)}}. \] Since their rolls are independent, the tie probability at the interior integer average \(\mu = 1\) behaves as: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R_1, R_2) \sim \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma^2 \sqrt{R_1 R_2}} = \frac{3}{4\pi \sqrt{R_1 R_2}}. \]

Thus, for a 3-sided die on \(V = \{0, 1, 2\}\), if \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) are coprime, a tie is still possible and decays as \(\Theta(R^{-1})\)! This is because the interior integer \(1\) is inside the support and can be achieved in many ways.

5.2.2 The Impact of Non-Uniform Supports

What if the support is non-uniform, say \(V = \{0, 2, 5\}\)? The range of averages is \([0, 5]\). The integers in the range are \(0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\). But are these integers achievable? Let \(R_1 = 100\) and \(R_2 = 101\): * To achieve average \(\mu = 1\), we need \(2n_2 + 5n_3 = R_1 = 100\) (achievable, e.g. \(n_2=50, n_3=0\)) and \(2m_2 + 5m_3 = R_2 = 101\). Let’s check if \(2m_2 + 5m_3 = 101\) is solvable in non-negative integers such that \(m_2 + m_3 \le 101\). Since \(101\) is odd, \(m_3\) must be odd. Let \(m_3 = 1\). Then \(2m_2 = 96 \implies m_2 = 48\). Since \(48 + 1 = 49 \le 101\), this is solvable! * What if the support is \(V = \{0, 3, 5\}\)? To achieve average \(1\), we need \(3n_2 + 5n_3 = 100\) and \(3m_2 + 5m_3 = 101\). For \(3n_2 + 5n_3 = 100\): let \(n_3 = 20 \implies n_2 = 0\). Solvable! For \(3m_2 + 5m_3 = 101\): let \(m_3 = 1 \implies 3m_2 = 96 \implies m_2 = 32\). Solvable! However, if we shift the support values such that the Diophantine equations have no non-negative integer solutions in \([0, R_1]\) and \([0, R_2]\), then the tie probability for that average is strictly zero. For non-uniform supports, ties are driven to be almost impossible because most interior integer averages are blocked Diophantinely.

5.3 Section 4.4: The General \(n\)-Sided Die with Unequal Tosses

We now generalize this behavior to arbitrary \(n\)-sided systems with supports \(V_1, V_2\) and probabilities $ ec{p}, ec{q}$.

Let \(g = \gcd(R_1, R_2)\). The set of shared tie averages is the intersection of the sets of achievable averages: \[ \mathcal{M} = \left\{\, \frac{m}{g} \in [v_{\min}, v_{\max}] \cap [w_{\min}, w_{\max}] \;:\; m \in \mathbb{Z} \,\right\} \] subject to the Diophantine compatibility of sums. If \(g = 1\), the shared averages must be integers. The larger the number of sides \(n\) and the wider the supports, the more interior integer averages become available, which mitigates the complete suppression seen in the coin game. However, if the support spacing is highly sparse or asymmetric, the Diophantine constraints remain active, keeping the tie probability near zero.

6 The General \(n\)-Sided Case: Slow, Progressive Analytical Breakdown

We now generalize our mathematical machinery to arbitrary \(n\)-sided systems (\(S = n\)). To maintain deep structural symmetry and high undergraduate readability, we evaluate the general case step-by-step, building progressive complexity from fair uniform baselines to general mismatched supports and probabilities.

The general tie probability formula is: \[ c_{1,R}(s) = \sum_{\substack{\vec{n} \,:\, |\vec{n}|=R \\ \sum n_i v_i = s}} \binom{R}{n_1, \dots, n_n} \prod_{i=1}^n p_i^{n_i}, \qquad c_{2,R}(s) = \sum_{\substack{\vec{m} \,:\, |\vec{m}|=R \\ \sum m_i w_i = s}} \binom{R}{m_1, \dots, m_n} \prod_{i=1}^n q_i^{m_i}, \] \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{s} c_{1,R}(s) \, c_{2,R}(s). \]

6.1 Case 1: The Fair \(n\)-Sided Die (\(p_i = q_i = 1/n\))

We begin with the symmetric baseline: both players roll from the standard, uniformly spaced value set \(V = \{0, 1, \dots, n-1\}\) with uniform probabilities \(p_i = 1/n\). The probability weights simplify to: \[ c_R(s) = n^{-R} \sum_{\substack{\vec{n} \,:\, |\vec{n}|=R \\ \sum n_i i = s}} \binom{R}{n_1, \dots, n_n}. \] These sum weights correspond to the multinomial coefficients of \((1 + y + y^2 + \dots + y^{n-1})^R\). The maximal lattice span of \(V\) is \(d = 1\) (Definition 5.1). The mean is \(\mu = (n-1)/2\), and the variance of a single roll is: \[ \sigma^2 = \frac{n^2 - 1}{12}. \] Applying the lattice Local Limit Theorem, we can derive the exact polynomial asymptotic decay constant:

Theorem 5.1. For two independent players rolling fair standard \(n\)-sided dice (\(p_i \equiv 1/n\)), the asymptotic tie probability is: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\pi R (n^2-1)}}. \]

Proof. By Theorem 5.7 (proven below), setting span \(d = 1\) and variance \(\sigma^2 = (n^2-1)/12\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \frac{n^2-1}{12}}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{\pi R (n^2-1)}}. \qquad \blacksquare \]

(Note: For \(n=2\), this yields \(\sqrt{3/3\pi R} = 1/\sqrt{\pi R}\), matching Case 1 of Section 1.2 exactly. For \(n=3\), it yields \(\sqrt{3/8\pi R}\), matching Case 1 of Section 3.2 exactly).

6.2 Case 2: Matched but Unfair \(n\)-Sided Die (\(\vec{p} = \vec{q} \neq \vec{1}/n\))

Now, both players use the same biased \(n\)-sided die on standard support \(V = \{0, 1, \dots, n-1\}\), sharing the same probability vector \(\vec{p}\). Since they share the same distribution, their expected values are identical, maintaining maximum overlap. By the Local Limit Theorem, the tie probability still decays polynomially as \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\), with the variance \(\sigma^2\) determined by the prior distribution \(\vec{p}\): \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}}, \qquad \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i i^2 - \left( \sum_{i=1}^n p_i i \right)^2. \]

6.4 Case 4: Mismatched Values with Matching Probabilities (\(V_1 \neq V_2, \vec{p} = \vec{q}\))

Now, Player 1 rolls from \(V_1 = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}\) and Player 2 rolls from \(V_2 = \{w_1, \dots, w_n\}\), sharing the same prior probability vector \(\vec{p}\). Even though their probabilities match, because their supports differ, their prior expectations are unequal: \[ \mu_1 = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i v_i \neq \mu_2 = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i w_i. \] This mismatch in physical supports shifts their expectations and forces the tie probability to decay exponentially, illustrating that support mismatches act as physical entropy barriers just as probability mismatches do.

6.5 Case 5: The General \(n\)-Sided Mismatched Case (\(V_1 \neq V_2, \vec{p} \neq \vec{q}\))

Finally, we reach the most general \(n\)-sided configuration, with complete support and probability asymmetry. The general joint optimization is: \[ I^* = \min_{\vec{x}, \vec{y}} D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x} \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y} \| \vec{q}) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \sum_{i=1}^n x_i v_i = \sum_{i=1}^n y_i w_i = \mu. \] The optimal distributions are the generalized Gibbs (Boltzmann) distributions: \[ x_i^* = \frac{p_i e^{\lambda v_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^n p_j e^{\lambda v_j}}, \qquad y_i^* = \frac{q_i e^{-\lambda w_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^n q_j e^{-\lambda w_j}} \] where \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\) is the unique Lagrange multiplier aligning their averages. By Theorem 3.1, the exponential decay rate is \(P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \Theta(e^{-I^* R})\). The greater the distance in probability shapes (\(P\)) and physical ranges (\(V\)), the larger the joint minimum KL cost \(D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{x}^* \| \vec{p}) + D_{\text{KL}}(\vec{y}^* \| \vec{q})\) becomes, accelerating the exponential decay.

6.6 Case 6: The \(n\)-Sided Collision Lattice and Geometric Projections

To study collisions on general \(n\)-sided supports, we define the lattice of composition changes:

Definition 5.2 (Maximal Lattice Spacing / Span). Let \(D\) be a discrete random variable supported on a set of integers \(V = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}\). The span \(d\) of the distribution is defined as: \[ d = \gcd(v_2 - v_1, v_3 - v_2, \dots, v_n - v_{n-1}). \]

Definition 5.3. For a value set \(V = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}\), the collision lattice \(L(V)\) is: \[ L(V) = \left\{\, \vec{d} \in \mathbb{Z}^n \;:\; \sum_{i=1}^n d_i = 0, \ \ \sum_{i=1}^n d_i v_i = 0 \,\right\}. \]

\(L(V)\) is the kernel of the linear map \(\mathbb{Z}^n \to \mathbb{Z}^2\), \(\vec{d} \mapsto \left( \sum d_i, \sum d_i v_i \right)\), and is therefore a sublattice of \(\mathbb{Z}^n\).

Lemma 5.4. Two compositions \(\vec{n}, \vec{n}'\) of size \(R\) produce identical sums if and only if \(\vec{n} - \vec{n}' \in L(V)\).

Proposition 5.5. If \(v_1, \dots, v_n\) are not all equal, then \(\operatorname{rank} L(V) = n - 2\).

Proof. The linear map \(\phi: \mathbb{Z}^n \to \mathbb{Z}^2\), \(\vec{d} \mapsto \left(\sum d_i, \sum d_i v_i \right)\) has an image of rank \(2\) since the values \(v_i\) are not all equal. By the rank-nullity theorem, the kernel of \(\phi\), which is exactly \(L(V)\), has rank: \[ \operatorname{rank} L(V) = n - \operatorname{rank}(\operatorname{im} \phi) = n - 2. \qquad \blacksquare \]

Corollary 5.6. For every \(n \geq 3\) and every choice of \(n\) distinct values, there exists \(R_0(V)\) such that for all \(R \geq R_0\), some sum-class \(s\) receives contributions from at least two distinct compositions.

6.6.1 Resolution and Proof of Conjecture 8.2 (Now Theorem 5.7)

We can formally prove Conjecture 8.2 using the Local Limit Theorem for Lattice Distributions.

Theorem 5.7. Let \(V\) consist of \(n\) values with maximal span \(d\) (Definition 5.2) and let \(p_i \equiv 1/n\). Let \(\sigma^2\) be the single-roll variance. As \(R \to \infty\), the tie probability decays polynomially as: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{d}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}}. \] In particular, \(P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \Theta(R^{-1/2})\), independent of the number of sides \(n\).

Proof. Let \(S_R\) be the sum of \(R\) rolls. Since \(D\) has maximal span \(d\) (Definition 5.2), \(S_R\) is a lattice random variable supported on the integers with spacing \(d\). By the multidimensional Local Limit Theorem (or the 1D LLT for lattice distributions), the probability mass function of the sum \(S_R\) is asymptotically: \[ c_R(s) = \Pr(S_R = s) = \frac{d}{\sqrt{2\pi R \sigma^2}} \exp\left( -\frac{(s - R\mu)^2}{2 R \sigma^2} \right) + o\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{R}}\right). \] The tie probability is the sum of squares: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_{s} c_R(s)^2 \sim \sum_{s} \frac{d^2}{2\pi R \sigma^2} \exp\left( -\frac{(s - R\mu)^2}{R \sigma^2} \right). \] Viewing this as a Riemann sum with step size \(\Delta x = \frac{d}{\sqrt{R\sigma^2}}\) and integrating: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{d}{2\pi \sqrt{R\sigma^2}} \sum_{s} \exp\left( -\frac{(s - R\mu)^2}{R\sigma^2} \right) \frac{d}{\sqrt{R\sigma^2}} \sim \frac{d}{2\pi \sqrt{R\sigma^2}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx. \] Since \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}\), we have: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) \sim \frac{d \sqrt{\pi}}{2\pi \sqrt{R\sigma^2}} = \frac{d}{\sqrt{4\pi R \sigma^2}}. \qquad \blacksquare \]

6.6.2 Geometric “Ellipsoid Projection” Interpretation

To provide an intuitive geometric picture of why \(P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \Theta(R^{-1/2})\) for all \(n\) (the result of Theorem 5.7), we analyze the geometry of the composition space and its projection:

  1. The Composition Simplex: For \(n\) sides and \(R\) rolls, the set of all possible composition vectors \(\vec{n} = (n_1, \dots, n_n)\) lies on an \((n-1)\)-dimensional simplex in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) defined by \(\sum n_i = R\). The number of integer points on this simplex is \(\binom{R+n-1}{n-1} = \Theta(R^{n-1})\).
  2. The Sum Projection: The sum map \(\sigma(\vec{n}) = \sum n_i v_i\) acts as a linear projection from the \((n-1)\)-dimensional composition space to the 1D sum space. The collision lattice \(L(V)\) of rank \(n-2\) represents the \((n-2)\)-dimensional kernel of this projection. Thus, each achievable sum \(s\) corresponds to an \((n-2)\)-dimensional slice (fiber) of the simplex.
  3. The Concentration Ellipsoid: By the Multidimensional Central Limit Theorem, the probability mass is not spread evenly across the simplex. Instead, as \(R \to \infty\), the probability mass concentrates in an \((n-1)\)-dimensional Gaussian ellipsoid centered at the expectation \(\vec{\mu}_R = R \vec{p}\) with standard deviation scaling as \(\sqrt{R}\) in all directions. The volume of this concentration ellipsoid is of order \(O(R^{(n-1)/2})\).
  4. Projecting the Ellipsoid: When we project this concentration ellipsoid onto the 1D sum axis, the image is an interval of length proportional to the standard deviation of the sum, which is \(O(\sqrt{R})\). Within this interval, there are only \(O(\sqrt{R} / d)\) active sum-lattice classes.
  5. Collision Densities: Since the total probability mass inside the ellipsoid is close to 1, and this mass is projected onto a 1D interval containing \(O(R^{1/2})\) sum classes, the average probability \(c_R(s)\) of each active sum class must be of order \(\Theta(R^{-1/2})\). The sum of squares (collision probability) is therefore: \[ P_{\text{tie}}(R) = \sum_s c_R(s)^2 = \Theta(R^{1/2}) \times \Theta\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{R}}\right)^2 = \Theta\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{R}}\right). \]

This geometric picture reveals why the number of sides \(n\) does not increase the decay rate: as \(n\) grows, the composition simplex and the concentration ellipsoid grow in dimensionality, but they are always projected down to a one-dimensional sum axis of width \(O(\sqrt{R})\). The dimension of the fiber (\(n-2\)) exactly matches the extra degrees of freedom in the composition ellipsoid, meaning the collision probability is always governed purely by the 1D width of the projection.

The generating function and Local Limit Theorem tools already applied to any \(n\).
